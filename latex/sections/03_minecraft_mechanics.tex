\section{Minecraft as a Construction Substrate}
\label{sec:minecraft-mechanics}

A central claim of this work is that building a circuit inside Minecraft constitutes a
meaningfully different experience from constructing one in a schematic tool. Substantiating
that claim requires describing precisely what ``building'' means within Minecraft itself,
and identifying which of the game's own mechanics the mod reuses, extends, or deliberately
avoids.

\subsection{The voxel world and block placement}

Minecraft's world is a three-dimensional grid of one-metre cubes, or \emph{blocks}; nearly
every action available to a player -- constructing a building, excavating a mine,
redirecting a river -- reduces to placing and removing blocks within this grid. A block
occupies exactly one grid cell and, depending on its type, may carry a small amount of state
directly within that cell (its \emph{block state}): a small enumeration such as which of the
six axis-aligned directions it is \emph{facing}, whether a door is open, or how much liquid
a cauldron holds. When a player places a block possessing a facing property, the game
records the direction the player was looking at the moment of placement as that block's
initial facing; the block may subsequently be rotated using other in-game tools, but its
facing otherwise remains fixed until deliberately changed. This mechanic, in which an
object's orientation is set once, at placement time, and persists until deliberately
altered, is precisely what every two-terminal component in this mod reuses to determine
which two of its six faces serve as its electrical leads (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}): a
resistor placed while facing north has its leads on its north and south faces, because that
is already the behavior of the base game's own oriented blocks (a piston, a dispenser, a
chest with a facing hinge). Consequently, no new placement mechanic had to be introduced for
players already familiar with the base game.

\subsection{Redstone: Minecraft's native circuit system}

The base game already provides an entire circuit-like subsystem, redstone, built around
\emph{redstone dust} (a wire-like block that carries a signal), \emph{redstone torches} and
other sources (which emit a signal), and a large family of blocks that read an incoming
signal to trigger an effect such as opening a door or activating a piston. A redstone signal
is an integer strength from 0 to 15 that decreases by one for every block of dust it
traverses, and updates propagate through a discrete neighbor-notification event fired once
per game tick rather than continuously; the system has no built-in notion of voltage,
current, capacitance, resistance, or continuous time. Despite this limitation, redstone is
widely used, both informally and as a subject of academic
interest~\cite{nebel2016mining}, as an entry point into digital logic and rudimentary
computer architecture: players construct logic gates, adders, and working memory cells
entirely from redstone components, and it is common for players to describe and reason
about redstone circuits in explicitly electrical language despite the underlying mechanic
being discrete and boolean rather than continuous~\cite{dezuanni2015redstone}. This
observation motivates a specific design decision in this mod: Minecraft's building metaphor
already appears to prime players to reason electrically, yet redstone itself cannot serve as
the vehicle for teaching \emph{analog} circuit behavior, since it possesses no continuous
quantity to teach with. Community-developed ``analog redstone'' techniques exist that
approximate a continuous value by quantizing it into a comparator's 0--15 signal strength,
but these yield only a coarse, sixteen-level discretization rather than a continuously
varying, numerically accurate quantity.

\subsection{Block entities and the tick loop}

A plain block's state is limited to the small, fixed set of properties declared for its
block type -- sufficient for a facing direction or an open/closed flag, but not for an
arbitrary amount of mutable data such as a capacitor's stored charge or 200 samples of
recent voltage history. Minecraft's modding interfaces expose a second, richer construct for
precisely this situation, the \emph{block entity}: an object associated with a specific
block position that may hold arbitrary data and, if the block type requests it, is given an
opportunity to execute code once per game tick (20 times per second, the same rate at which
the remainder of the game is simulated). Every component, wire node, ground reference, and
configuration module in this mod is implemented as a block entity for precisely this reason:
it is the mechanism the platform already provides for a block that must retain state and
act upon it regularly, and reusing it ensures that the mod's simulation cadence is
naturally locked to the game's own tick rate rather than requiring a separate timing
mechanism.

\subsection{What the mod adds}

Given the preceding discussion, the mod's own contribution is deliberately narrow: it
introduces a second, parallel notion of adjacency (whether a given face is electrically
conductive toward its neighbor, Section~\ref{sec:architecture}) alongside the game's
existing physical adjacency, and it supplies the resulting graph to a genuine solver every
tick rather than encoding behavior directly into the blocks themselves. Redstone is reused
deliberately, and only for the single concept it is genuinely well suited to represent in
this mod: a boolean on/off switch gating whether a voltage source is connected at all
(Section~\ref{sec:pedagogy}), rather than being pressed into service as an analog signal it
was never designed to carry.
